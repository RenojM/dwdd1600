About The Rider

Motorcycle Sterotypes

What you ride says about you.

Yamaha Riders
This group is a mixed bag of ego and expertise. Most Yamaha sport bike riders (if they aren't on a newer R1) are convinced they're the fastest thing on the road—even if it's mostly in their head—and there's a 90% chance they're "car guys" when they aren't on two wheels. If you see a 2006-2008 R6, it's likely being used as a prop by an Instagram model for glamour shots and page views rather than actual canyon carving. However, the 2015+ R1 rider is the exception; they're usually a middle-aged track junkie who knows exactly what the hell they're doing and doesn't care that the front end looks a little bizarre.
Honda CBR Riders
This group is usually defined by either practicality or parents with deep pockets. The CBF600 and CBR650f riders are those who want to carve canyons and commute on a sport bike, but they're often battling lower back problems. Then you have the CBR300 and CBR500 crowd, who mainly bought the bike because Honda makes great cars and they simply walked away with whatever the floor salesman sold them. Finally, the CBR600rr is the classic choice for the 17 year old rich kid whose parents had to convince the sales rep he could handle a 600 as a first bike just because he rode dirt bikes as a kid.
Kawasaki Riders
The Green Team usually falls into two camps: the aspiring motovlogger or the high-interest adventurer. Many Ninja riders started because of a YouTube recommendation; they'll often claim they've "outgrown" the bike in under a year, despite having wide, dry chicken strips on their tires that say otherwise. You'll also find the ZX series crowd, frequently purchased with an enlistment bonus at a 16% APR. From the ZX14 riders to the Ninja 650 owners who wish they'd bought an SV650 instead, this group is the unofficial patron saint of the "spray-paint cosmetic overhaul.
Harley-Davidson Riders
Post-2004 Model Riders: This is often the 48-year-old accountant who is desperately trying to recapture his masculinity. You'll find him on the weekends "chasing the spirit of the road" as a way to escape the office and feel like an outlaw for a few hours.Pre-2004 Model Riders: This group tends to be a bit grittier. You're either looking at a hipster who loves the vintage aesthetic or a sketchy guy who has probably seen some things he shouldn't have. This is the rider who likely encourages his 52-year-old girlfriend to enter wet t-shirt contests at the local biker bar just for the hell of it.

Rider Mindset

The Flow State

  • Riding serves as a form of moving meditation that anchors the mind in the present moment, allowing daily stresses and anxieties to fade away.
  • It induces a "flow state" where the rider is completely immersed in the activity, resulting in peak performance and a sense of time standing stil
  • Navigating challenging conditions builds a unique self-reliance and confidence that empowers riders to tackle other life challenges with a positive attitude.

Calculated Risk and Laser Focus

  • The psychological thrill of motorcycling is rooted in a balance between unparalleled freedom and an acute perception of risk.
  • Skilled riders maintain a laser-like focus to constantly assess their environment, making split-second decisions that ensure personal agency.
  • Maintaining this mindset requires a commitment to safety, including wearing the right gear and continuously honing technical skills to protect the community.
